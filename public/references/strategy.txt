Goal

Take a time-ordered list of notes and produce a playable sequence of fret/string placements that balances left-hand comfort and right-hand economy/sustain.
Inputs (conceptual)

Notes: pitch, time, duration.
Instrument: tuning, strings, fretboard limits.
Two human-facing knobs: “left‑hand economy” (favor compact, low-movement shapes) and “right‑hand economy / sustain” (favor holding strings, minimizing pick travel, keeping notes ringing).
Playstyle hint: pick vs fingerstyle (optional).
High-level steps

Normalize the music
Turn the song into discrete time slices (group notes that sound together or nearly together).
Keep note durations so you know which notes will still be ringing later.
Produce plausible fingerings for each slice
For every note in a slice, list the reasonable places on the neck it could be played.
Combine those into a manageable set of candidate fingerings for that slice, discarding obviously impossible or unhelpful shapes.
Evaluate each candidate in plain language
For any single candidate ask:
“Is this shape compact and comfortable for the left hand?”
“Does it use open strings or barres in a way that helps or hurts?”
“Will this shape let long notes keep ringing?”
“Would the right hand need to move far to play from here?”
Use the two knobs (left‑hand economy and right‑hand economy/sustain) to decide how much each of these questions matters.
Evaluate transitions between consecutive slices
For two neighboring shapes ask:
“How far must the left hand move to get from A to B?”
“Will choosing B mute notes that were supposed to keep ringing?”
“Will the right hand have to jump to a very different string or region?”
Prefer transitions that preserve useful holds, reduce big jumps, and fit the declared playstyle.
Pick a globally good sequence
Consider the whole song and choose the sequence of candidate shapes that gives the best overall balance of left‑hand comfort and right‑hand sustain across time. That means sometimes picking a slightly worse shape now to avoid a big move later, or holding a shape so a long note sustains.
The selection is done by searching through the candidate shapes and their transitions to find the best overall path from start to finish.
Post-process and label
Clean up tiny impractical moves (small oscillations).
Mark techniques like hammer‑on, pull‑off, tap when they naturally arise from the chosen fingerings and timing.
Optionally produce a small set of alternatives that trade more sustain for less left‑hand motion or vice versa.
Outputs

A mapped song: each note annotated with string and fret, plus technique hints.
A human summary: whether the result favors left‑hand comfort or sustain and any spots that required awkward compromises.
Design notes (intended behavior, not implementation)

Hard rules first: discard impossible fingerings early (you don’t try to make the impossible playable).
Knobs guide priorities: turn up left‑hand economy to reduce hand travel; turn up sustain to keep notes ringing and reduce right‑hand movement.
Global thinking beats local greed: sometimes accept a locally awkward shape if it saves big moves later or preserves long notes.
Keep candidate sets small and sensible: you don’t try every theoretical possibility, only musically reasonable ones.
Optional modes (user-facing)

“Comfort mode” — maximize left‑hand ease; may sacrifice a bit of sustain.
“Sustain mode” — keep long tones ringing and minimize right‑hand travel; may produce more barre shapes or larger left‑hand spans.
“Balanced” — default compromise.
